POSSE & Webmentions on a Static Quarto Site

IndieWeb
Quarto

Authors

Claude Sonnet 4.6

Published

Abstract

The IndieWeb movement offers a compelling answer to social media fatigue: own your content, syndicate it outward, and let the web itself carry the conversation back to you. In this post I walk through how I implemented POSSE and Webmentions on this site — a fully static Quarto website hosted on GitHub Pages — with no backend, no database, and no third-party JavaScript framework.

The problem with social media as infrastructure

Every time you publish a thought on LinkedIn, a thread on a forum, or a reply on a social platform, you are building on rented land. The platform controls discovery, the platform controls the archive, and the platform can — and does — change the rules. Accounts get suspended, APIs get restricted, companies get acquired.

The IndieWeb movement’s answer is simple and old: own your content. Publish first on a domain you control, then push copies outward. The acronym for this workflow is POSSE: Publish on your Own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere.

But owning the canonical post is only half the equation. If someone replies to your LinkedIn copy, that conversation is trapped on LinkedIn. Webmentions close that loop: they are a W3C-standardised protocol that lets any website notify another that it has linked to or responded to it. Combined, POSSE + Webmentions give you a personal site that is the source of truth and an active participant in distributed conversations.

How POSSE works

The POSSE workflow is deliberately low-tech:

  1. Write and publish on your own site first. The URL on your domain is the canonical URL — the one you want search engines, archives, and readers to remember.
  2. Syndicate copies to platforms — LinkedIn, Mastodon, a newsletter, an aggregator — each copy links back to the original with a rel="canonical" or just a plain “Originally published at…” line.
  3. Collect responses. Ideally, replies on those platforms flow back to your site via Webmentions (more on that below). At minimum, you know the conversation happened because you own the thread.

The key insight is the direction of authority: the copy points to the original, never the other way around. Your domain is the source; social media platforms are distribution channels.

For this site, the technical foundation of POSSE is already in place:

  • site-url: https://ggilles.dev in _quarto.yml — Quarto embeds this as the canonical base URL in every page’s <head>, so syndicated copies can always point home.
  • RSS feed at /blog.xml — any reader or aggregator can subscribe directly without going through an algorithm.

How Webmentions work

Webmention (W3C Recommendation, 2017) is a simple HTTP protocol. When site B publishes a post that links to a URL on site A, site B’s publishing software sends a POST request to site A’s webmention endpoint. Site A receives the notification, verifies that the link is real, and can display it however it likes.

The full flow looks like this:

Site B publishes a post linking to https://ggilles.dev/posts/my-post/
  ↓
Site B (or a service) POSTs to https://webmention.io/ggilles.dev/webmention
  with source=https://siteB.com/their-post & target=https://ggilles.dev/posts/my-post/
  ↓
webmention.io verifies the link exists in site B's page
  ↓
webmention.io stores the mention
  ↓
ggilles.dev fetches the mention from the webmention.io API and displays it

Three actors are involved:

Actor Role
webmention.io Receives and stores incoming webmentions for your domain
Bridgy Polls social platforms (LinkedIn, Mastodon…) and converts likes, reposts, and replies into webmentions
Your site Declares the endpoint; fetches and renders the stored mentions

Implementation on a static Quarto site

A static site cannot receive HTTP POST requests, which is why webmention.io exists: it acts as your inbox. Your site only needs to advertise the endpoint in its <head> and fetch the stored data via a read API.

Step 1 — Declare your identity and endpoint

Create assets/head-indieweb.html:

<!-- IndieWeb: webmention endpoint -->
<link rel="webmention" href="https://webmention.io/ggilles.dev/webmention" />
<link rel="pingback"   href="https://webmention.io/ggilles.dev/xmlrpc" />

<!-- IndieAuth: rel="me" identity links -->
<link rel="me" href="https://github.com/guillaumegilles" />
<link rel="me" href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/guillaumegilles" />

The rel="webmention" tag tells any Webmention-aware sender where to deliver notifications. The rel="me" tags are used by IndieAuth to verify that the profiles listed belong to the owner of ggilles.dev — a prerequisite for signing in to webmention.io without a password.1

1 IndieAuth works by cross-referencing: your site links to your GitHub profile with rel="me", and your GitHub profile links back to your site. This mutual link proves ownership.

Add this file to _quarto.yml so it is injected into every page:

format:
  html:
    include-in-header: assets/head-indieweb.html

Step 2 — Sign up at webmention.io

Go to webmention.io and sign in with your site’s URL. webmention.io will look for the rel="me" links in your <head> and verify your identity via IndieAuth. Once confirmed, it starts accepting mentions addressed to your domain.

This is a one-time, zero-configuration step. webmention.io is free for personal sites.

Step 3 — Wire up Bridgy for social backfeeding

Bridgy is the bridge between siloed social platforms and the open web. Connect it to your LinkedIn, Mastodon, or other accounts and it will:

  1. Poll those platforms for likes, reposts, and replies on posts that link to your site.
  2. Convert each interaction into a Webmention and send it to webmention.io.

No code required — it is a point-and-click setup on brid.gy. Once connected, social interactions flow automatically into your webmention.io inbox.

Step 4 — Display webmentions on posts

The webmention.io read API is public. A small JavaScript snippet — included once and applied to every post — fetches mentions for the current page URL and renders them.

Create assets/webmentions.html:

<section id="webmentions">
  <h2>Webmentions</h2>
  <div id="wm-facepile"></div>
  <div id="wm-replies"></div>
  <p id="wm-empty" style="display:none;">No webmentions yet.</p>
</section>

<script>
(function () {
  const LIKES   = ["like-of", "bookmark-of"];
  const REPOSTS = ["repost-of"];
  const REPLIES = ["in-reply-to", "mention-of"];

  const target = encodeURIComponent(window.location.href);
  const api    = `https://webmention.io/api/mentions.jf2?target=${target}&per-page=100`;

  fetch(api)
    .then(r => r.json())
    .then(data => {
      const mentions = data.children || [];
      if (!mentions.length) {
        document.getElementById("wm-empty").style.display = "block";
        return;
      }
      const facepile  = document.getElementById("wm-facepile");
      const repliesEl = document.getElementById("wm-replies");

      mentions.forEach(m => {
        const type   = m["wm-property"];
        const author = m.author || {};
        // render likes/reposts as avatars, replies as cards
        // … (see full source below)
      });
    });
})();
</script>

The API returns a JF2 feed — a JSON format for social interactions. Each entry has a wm-property field that describes the interaction type:

wm-property Meaning
like-of Someone liked the post
bookmark-of Someone bookmarked it
repost-of Someone reposted or shared it
in-reply-to A direct reply
mention-of The post was mentioned but not directly replied to

Likes and reposts are rendered as a compact facepile — a row of avatar images. Replies and mentions get a full reply card with the author’s name, date, and a preview of their text.

Tell Quarto to append this widget to every post by adding one line to posts/_metadata.yml:

format:
  html:
    include-after-body: ../assets/webmentions.html

_metadata.yml in a subdirectory applies to all documents in that directory and its children — so this single line adds the widget to every post without touching individual .qmd files.

The full picture

Here is how all the pieces fit together in the final architecture:

You write a post → quarto render → push to GitHub Pages
                                         │
                    ┌────────────────────┘
                    │ <link rel="webmention" href="webmention.io/…" />
                    │ <link rel="me" href="github.com/guillaumegilles" />
                    │ RSS feed at /blog.xml
                    └→ ggilles.dev (canonical source)

You share the post URL on LinkedIn / Mastodon
                    └→ Bridgy polls LinkedIn
                         └→ sends Webmention to webmention.io
                              └→ stored in webmention.io inbox

Reader opens a post on ggilles.dev
                    └→ webmentions.html JS runs
                         └→ GET webmention.io/api/mentions.jf2?target=…
                              └→ renders likes, reposts, replies inline

No server, no database, no build-time API calls. The canonical content is fully static; social interactions are fetched client-side on demand.

Sending webmentions when you publish

Receiving webmentions is automatic once the endpoint is declared. Sending them — notifying other sites that you have linked to them — requires an extra step.

The simplest approach is webmention.app: a free service that polls your RSS feed for new posts and sends webmentions to every URL you link to. Point it at https://ggilles.dev/blog.xml and it handles the rest.

Alternatively, you can trigger sends manually with the webmention CLI or add a GitHub Actions workflow that runs on every push to main.

What does not require any of this

POSSE does not require Webmentions to be useful. Even without the feedback loop, the discipline of publishing on your own domain first — and treating social platforms as mirrors rather than originals — gives you:

  • A permanent, searchable archive you control.
  • A canonical URL to link to from anywhere.
  • Independence from any single platform’s algorithm or terms of service.

Webmentions are the layer that makes the distributed conversation visible. They are a nice-to-have that upgrades the experience significantly if the people engaging with your content are on IndieWeb-aware platforms (Mastodon, personal blogs running WordPress with the Webmention plugin, etc.).

LinkedIn and most corporate platforms will likely never implement Webmention natively — that is what Bridgy is for.

Replicating this on your own Quarto site

The full implementation is four files and two external accounts:

Files

File Purpose
assets/head-indieweb.html Webmention endpoint + rel="me" identity
assets/webmentions.html Client-side display widget
posts/_metadata.yml Auto-include widget on all posts
_quarto.yml include-in-header, site-url

External accounts (both free)

Service What it does
webmention.io Your webmention inbox
brid.gy Social platform backfeeding

Replace every occurrence of ggilles.dev with your own domain and every social profile URL with yours. The rest is generic and reusable as-is.
